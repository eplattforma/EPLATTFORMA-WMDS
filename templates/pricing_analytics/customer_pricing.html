What this report shows

Stale Pricing finds items a customer used to buy, but has not bought recently, and compares the last price the customer paid to a current market reference price (based on other customers). This helps sales identify items that are good candidates for a win-back offer.

Stale Min Days / Stale Max Days

Defines when the customer last bought the item. Example: Min 180 / Max 400 shows items whose last purchase was 180–400 days ago.

Market Days

Controls how the system calculates the market reference price. Example: Market Days = 90 uses sales from the last 90 days.

Ref Current (Market Reference Price)

Ref Current is calculated from other customers’ recent purchases (not the selected customer). For each customer who bought the item in the Market Days window, the system calculates that customer’s average unit price:
unit price = SUM(net_excl) / SUM(qty)
Then the system takes either the Market Median or Market Max across those customer prices.

How to interpret
  • Last Price > Ref Current: customer’s last price is above today’s market → price may be a reason they stopped buying.
  • Last Price < Ref Current: customer previously paid a good price → likely not a price issue (substitution, needs, stock, etc.).
Suggested price (if shown)

The suggested win-back price is conservative (usually min(Last Price, Ref Current)).

Note: For seasonal/discontinued items, “stale” may be normal.

Price Index vs Market — Help

What this report does
Price Index compares the prices a customer paid for items in the selected period against a market reference price calculated from your own customer base.

For each item we show:
Customer Price = total net sales ÷ total quantity for that customer (excl VAT)
Market Price = the benchmark price for the same item (Median or Max)
Index = Customer Price ÷ Market Price
Δ €/unit and Δ € total = how far the customer is from the benchmark price

How “Market Median” is calculated
Market Median is calculated in two steps, per item:
1. Customer-average price per item: For each customer who bought the item in the period, we calculate: customer_item_price = SUM(net_excl) / SUM(qty)
2. Median across customers: We then take the median of those customer-average prices: market_median = median(customer_item_price across customers)
✅ This means each customer counts once, and the median is robust (a single extreme price does not distort the benchmark).

What is included / excluded
By default, Pricing Analytics uses positive sales only:
Included: lines where qty > 0 and net_excl > 0
Excluded: credit notes / returns (usually negative lines), zero-value lines, and zero-qty lines
If you tick Include credits, returns/credits are included for the customer totals (but market benchmarks still use positive sales only).

Why Price Index revenue may be lower than Customer 360
Price Index benchmarks only the Top N items (default Top 50) by customer revenue for performance and clarity. So the “Revenue” shown in Price Index is the revenue covered by those Top N items, not the customer’s full sales.

Use:
Gross Positive Sales (all items) = total positive sales for the customer in the period
Benchmarked Revenue (Top N) = the part included in Price Index
Coverage % = Benchmarked Revenue ÷ Gross Positive Sales

How to interpret the Index
Index = 1.00 → customer price is at the market reference
Index > 1.00 → customer price is higher than benchmark
Index < 1.00 → customer price is lower than benchmark

Benchmark selector (Median vs Max)
Median = “typical market” (recommended default for fair comparison)
Max = “ceiling observed in market” (useful for headroom checks; can be influenced by outliers)

PVM — Help (Price / Volume / Mix)

What is PVM?
PVM explains why a customer’s revenue changed between a Current period and a Baseline period (Previous Period or Prior Year). It breaks the change into Price, Volume, and Mix.

P = Price effect
Revenue change caused by unit price changes on items the customer already bought before.

V = Volume effect
Revenue change caused by buying more or fewer units (quantity changes), assuming baseline prices.

M = Mix effect
Revenue change caused by a different basket: new items bought now (not in baseline), lost items, or a shift to different items.

How are the numbers calculated?
For each item we compute:
P Curr = revenue / qty (current), P Base = revenue / qty (baseline)
Then:
ΔRevenue = R1 − R0
Volume effect = P0 × (Q1 − Q0)
Price effect = Q1 × (P1 − P0)
New items effect = Revenue from items not in baseline

Mix vs New items
In this report, we intentionally avoid the confusing “interaction” term. For items bought in both periods (COMMON), ΔRevenue is explained only by Price and Volume. The “New items effect” column is used only for items that appear in the current period but not in the baseline (NEW).

How to interpret common cases

  • New / Lost / Common items: If an item was not purchased in the baseline period, it is treated as NEW and appears under Mix. If an item was purchased in the baseline period but not in the current period, it is treated as LOST and appears under Volume (lost volume at baseline price). Price/Volume effects are most meaningful on COMMON items (bought in both periods).
  • Mix dominates: Many items have Q Base = 0 (new items) or Q Curr = 0 (lost items). This means the basket changed.
  • Price dominates: Unit prices changed on existing items (discounts/pricelist changes).
  • Volume dominates: The customer bought more/less quantity of the same items.

Net vs Gross (Credit Notes)
The headline “Δ Net Revenue” includes credit notes/returns.
PVM (Price/Volume/Mix) explains the change in Gross Positive Sales only (normal sales lines).
The difference between Net and Gross is shown as Credits/Returns effect.

Small price changes filter
The report can ignore tiny price changes (default: < €0.10 or < 0.5%) to avoid misleading “PRICE-driven” signals caused by rounding.

Tip: PVM requires Compare mode (Previous period or PY). If Compare is “None”, PVM cannot run.
