1) Help text (Stale Pricing) — copy/paste
Title

Stale Pricing (Win-Back Pricing)

Body

What this report shows
Stale Pricing finds items a customer used to buy, but has not bought recently, and compares the last price the customer paid to a current market reference price (based on other customers). This helps sales identify items that are good candidates for a win-back offer.

Stale Min Days / Stale Max Days
These define when the customer last bought the item.

If Stale Min = 180 and Stale Max = 400, the report shows items whose last purchase was 180–400 days ago.

If an item was purchased more recently than the Min Days threshold, it is not stale and will not appear.

Market Days
Market Days controls how the system calculates the Ref Current market price.

If Market Days = 90, the system looks at sales from the last 90 days.

Ref Current (Market Reference Price)
Ref Current is calculated from other customers’ recent purchases (not the selected customer).
For each customer who bought the item in the Market Days window, the system calculates that customer’s average unit price:

Then the market reference is computed as:

Market Median: median of customer average prices (robust benchmark)

Market Max: highest customer average price (upper benchmark)

How to interpret the result

If Last Price > Ref Current, the customer’s last price is higher than today’s market → price may be a reason they stopped buying.

If Last Price < Ref Current, they previously paid a good price → the reason they stopped is likely not price (substitution, needs changed, stock, etc.).

Suggested price (if shown)
The suggested price is a conservative win-back target, usually:

min(Last Price, Ref Current)
so you don’t propose a price higher than what they last paid and above the market benchmark.

Note
Stale results are strongest for steady consumables. For seasonal/discontinued items, “stale” may be normal.
